# Customer Persona Creation

## What a Persona Is (and Isn't)

A persona is a **research-backed archetype** representing a segment of real customers. It is not a fictional character you invent. It is not a demographic bucket. It is a decision-making model for your product, marketing, and sales teams.

A good persona answers: "Why would this specific type of person pay for this?"

---

## Research Methods (Before Writing)

Always build personas from data, not assumptions:

| Source | What to extract |
|--------|----------------|
| Customer interviews (5–10) | Goals, frustrations, decision process, exact language |
| Support tickets | Recurring problems, failure modes |
| Sales call recordings | Objections, use case descriptions, why they chose you |
| Churn analysis | Why people leave (reveals what they needed but didn't get) |
| Survey open-ends | Unprompted self-description of their problem |
| Review mining (G2, App Store) | What they praise and complain about in your category |

**Minimum viable research:** 5 customer interviews + 20 reviews before writing.

## The Full Persona Template

### Section 1 — Identity

```
Persona Name: [Give them a real-sounding first name, not "Marketer Mary"]
Title/Role: [Job title or life role — be specific]
Company/Context: [Company type, size, industry — or life situation if B2C]
Age Range: [Not a single age — a range that captures the archetype]
Location: [Relevant if geography affects behavior]
```

### Section 2 — Demographics

```
Education: [Relevant only if it affects how they consume information]
Income / Budget Authority: [Do they sign, approve, or influence spend?]
Tech savviness: [Power user / mainstream / reluctant adopter]
Reports to: [Who holds them accountable?]
Team size: [How many people are affected by their decisions?]
```

### Section 3 — Goals and Motivations

Answer from the persona's perspective:

```
Primary professional goal: What are they measured on? What does success look like?
Secondary goal: What do they want to be true 12 months from now?
Personal motivation: What do they want peers / their manager to think about them?
```

### Section 4 — Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

JTBD describes the underlying progress the persona is trying to make, not what they buy:

```
Functional job: "I need to [accomplish X] so that [outcome Y] happens."
Emotional job: "I want to feel [confident/in control/respected] when I [situation]."
Social job: "I want to be seen as [capable/innovative/reliable] by [audience]."
```

**Example:**
- Functional: "I need to consolidate project updates so my manager stops asking me for status reports."
- Emotional: "I want to feel on top of my work instead of constantly behind."
- Social: "I want my team to see me as organized and strategic, not chaotic."

### Section 5 — Pain Points

Structure by stage of their day/workflow:

```
Trigger pain: What situation first makes them aware they have a problem?
Search pain: What frustrates them when they try to solve it themselves?
Solution pain: What is wrong with the current tools or processes they use?
Outcome pain: What cost (time, money, reputation) does the unsolved problem create?
```

### Section 6 — Objections and Fears

What stops them from buying or switching:

```
Top 3 objections:
1. [e.g., "It costs too much for a team our size."]
2. [e.g., "Our team won't adopt a new tool."]
3. [e.g., "We already have something that 'kind of' does this."]

Hidden fear:
"What if I buy this and it doesn't work? What does that say about me?"
```

### Section 7 — Buying Behavior

```
How they discover solutions:
- [Google search / peer recommendation / LinkedIn / newsletter / conference]

Who is involved in the decision:
- Champion: [This persona]
- Influencer: [e.g., Head of IT reviews security]
- Decision maker: [e.g., VP must approve spend over $X]
- Blocker: [e.g., Finance needs ROI justification]

Decision timeline: [Days / weeks / months from problem to purchase]
What triggers urgency: [New project, team growth, audit, failed status quo, budget cycle]
What form of proof they need: [Case study, free trial, peer reference, ROI calculator]
```

### Section 8 — Preferred Communication

```
Content formats they trust: [Long-form articles / video / podcasts / peer forums]
Channels they actually use: [Specific subreddits, newsletters, Slack groups, events]
Tone they respond to: [Direct and data-driven / conversational / aspirational]
Phrases they use to describe their problem (exact words from interviews):
- "[quote 1]"
- "[quote 2]"
- "[quote 3]"
```

## One-Page Persona Summary (for Sharing)

After completing the full template, distill to a shareable card:

```
Name: [Name], [Title]
Company type: [X-employee SaaS / B2C parent / etc.]

Their world in one sentence:
"[Name] spends their days trying to [goal] but is constantly blocked by [problem]."

They hire our product to: [JTBD functional job]
Their biggest fear: [Objection / hidden fear]
We win when we say: "[Message that directly addresses their emotional job]"
We lose when we say: "[Phrase that triggers their top objection]"
```

---

## Multiple Persona Management

Most products need 2–3 personas max. More than that signals unclear positioning.

| Persona | Stage in Funnel | Key Message Angle |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| Primary | Buyer/Champion | Direct value, ROI |
| Secondary | End user | Ease, daily benefit |
| Tertiary | Economic buyer | Risk reduction, total cost |

Create separate messaging tracks per persona. The same feature means different things to each.

---

## Persona Validation Checklist

- [ ] Built from at least 5 real interviews or 20+ reviews, not invented
- [ ] Includes exact customer quotes in the language section
- [ ] JTBD stated functionally, emotionally, and socially
- [ ] Objections are specific (not "it's too expensive" but "it costs more than Notion which we currently use")
- [ ] Buying committee mapped if B2B (champion, influencer, blocker, decision maker)
- [ ] One-page summary written for team sharing
- [ ] Validated with sales or customer success team before distribution
- [ ] Scheduled for refresh (revisit every 6–12 months or after major product change)
