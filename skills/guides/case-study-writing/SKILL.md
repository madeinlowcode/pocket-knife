# Case Study Writing Guide

## What a Great Case Study Does
A case study is not a testimonial and not a product brochure. It is a story with a measurable ending. The reader — typically a prospect evaluating your product — should finish it thinking: "This company solved exactly my problem, and I can see the proof."

---

## Structure: The 5-Part Framework

### Part 1: Customer Profile (50–100 words)
Set the stage before the problem. Tell the reader who this customer is so they can self-identify.

Include:
- Company name and industry
- Company size (employees or revenue range — whatever is most relevant)
- What they do / their business model
- One-line description of their role in the market

Example:
> "Meridian Logistics is a 200-person freight brokerage operating across 12 US states. They specialize in last-mile delivery for e-commerce retailers and manage over 4,000 shipments per week."

### Part 2: The Challenge (150–200 words)
Describe the problem in the customer's own terms. This section earns trust from prospects who share the same pain.

Rules:
- Name the specific operational problem, not a vague frustration
- Quantify the pain where possible (hours lost, costs incurred, conversion rate, error rate)
- Include a quote from the customer naming the problem in their words
- Do not mention your product yet

Pain framing patterns:
- "Before [product], [customer] was spending X hours per week on..."
- "With [volume/scale], the existing process was breaking down in [specific way]..."
- "The team knew they needed to fix [problem], but previous attempts with [generic tool] hadn't worked because..."

### Part 3: The Solution (200–300 words)
Explain what the customer did — with your product as the enabler, not the hero.

Structure:
1. Why they chose your solution over alternatives (brief)
2. How they implemented it (key steps without excessive technical detail)
3. Which specific features addressed which specific pain points
4. Timeline to value — when did they start seeing results?

Rules:
- The customer's team is the hero; your product is their tool
- Be specific about features used — vague descriptions ("we used the platform") add no credibility
- Include an implementation quote if available: "The setup took about two weeks, and the support team walked us through every step."

### Part 4: The Results (200–250 words)
This is the most important section. Quantify everything.

#### Results Structure
```
PRIMARY METRIC    → The headline number (the one that goes in the title)
SECONDARY METRICS → 2–3 supporting numbers that paint the full picture
QUALITATIVE WINS  → What changed that can't be easily measured
```

**Results Checklist — include as many as available:**
- [ ] Time saved (hours per week, per campaign, per process)
- [ ] Cost reduced (absolute $ or %)
- [ ] Revenue or pipeline increased ($, %)
- [ ] Error rate / defect rate reduced (%)
- [ ] Speed increase (turnaround time, processing speed)
- [ ] Team size / headcount ratio (same output with fewer people, or more output with same team)
- [ ] Customer satisfaction / NPS improvement
- [ ] Adoption rate / engagement metric

**Formatting results for impact:**
- Use a callout box or bold stat display for the top 3 numbers
- Write each metric as a specific sentence, not a bullet of raw numbers
- "The team reduced manual data entry by 14 hours per week" is stronger than "14 hours saved"

### Part 5: The Quote + CTA
End with a strong quote and a clear next step.

**Quote selection criteria:**
- Must contain a specific outcome or feeling, not generic praise
- Weak: "We love the product. It's been really helpful."
- Strong: "We used to lose three hours every Monday morning reconciling reports. Now that's completely gone. We just open the dashboard."
- Attribute correctly: Full name, title, company name

**CTA options:**
- "See how [Your Product] can do the same for your team → [link]"
- "Talk to a specialist → [demo link]"
- "Read more customer stories → [link]"

## Metrics: How to Get Them from Customers

### Interview Questions That Surface Numbers
- "Before you started using [product], how long did [process] take?"
- "How many people were involved in that process vs. now?"
- "Can you estimate the cost of the problem you had before?"
- "What's your team's capacity now vs. six months ago?"
- "What metric did your manager care about most — and how has it changed?"

### When Customers Won't Share Exact Numbers
- Use ranges: "reduced processing time by 50–70%"
- Use relative comparisons: "cut their previous benchmark in half"
- Use time periods: "within the first 90 days, the team had recovered their implementation cost"
- Always get approval on specific numbers before publishing

## Storytelling Techniques

### Build Narrative Tension in Part 2
Do not jump straight to "here is the problem." Build to it:
1. Company context (what was growing / changing)
2. The decision point or forcing event (why the old process couldn't scale)
3. The specific pain (the actual problem)

### Use Sensory Language for Pain and Transformation
- Pain: "Every Monday, the team would manually pull 12 separate reports, copy them into a spreadsheet, and spend three hours making the numbers agree."
- Transformation: "Now that entire process runs automatically. The report is waiting in their inbox when they arrive."

### Chronological vs. Result-First Structures
- **Chronological**: Best for long-form content; builds narrative investment
- **Result-first**: Best for sales collateral; leads with the outcome, then explains how

Result-first example opening:
> "In 90 days, Meridian Logistics cut their dispatch processing time by 60% — without adding a single headcount. Here's how they did it."

## Format and Length

| Use Case                        | Recommended Length   |
|---------------------------------|----------------------|
| Website case study page         | 600–1000 words       |
| PDF sales collateral            | 1–2 pages (800–1200 words) |
| Email or nurture campaign       | 300–500 words (link to full) |
| One-pager for sales calls       | 1 page, visual-heavy |
| Video case study script         | 150–250 words (60–90 sec VO) |

## Case Study Writing Checklist
- [ ] Customer profile establishes who the reader should self-identify with
- [ ] Challenge section contains at least one quantified pain point
- [ ] Challenge section does not mention your product
- [ ] Solution section names specific features — not just the product category
- [ ] Results section contains at minimum one hard metric with a number
- [ ] At least one direct customer quote in the document
- [ ] Quote contains a specific outcome, not just generic praise
- [ ] CTA directs to next logical step in the buyer journey
- [ ] Customer has reviewed and approved all claims and quotes
- [ ] Case study title leads with the result, not the company name
