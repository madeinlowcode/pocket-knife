# Competitor Teardown Framework

## Purpose and Scope

A competitor teardown answers one question: **where is the gap between what the market offers and what customers actually need?** The output is not a description of competitors — it's a positioning map that tells you where to win.

---

## Phase 1 — Competitor Identification

### Tiers of Competition

**Direct competitors:** Same product category, same customer segment, same use case.

**Indirect competitors:** Different mechanism, same outcome. (Notion vs. email for knowledge management.)

**Status quo competitors:** The "do nothing" or "build in-house" option. Often the most important to understand.

### Discovery Methods

```
- Google: "[your category] software / tool / service" — first 3 pages
- G2, Capterra, Product Hunt: category listings
- Reddit / Slack communities: "what do you use for X?"
- Job postings: companies hiring for your role area often list tools required
- Customer interviews: "what did you use before us / what did you consider?"
- LinkedIn: search your category, filter by company size matching your ICP
```

Build a list of **5–10 competitors max**. Going wider dilutes your analysis. Prioritize:
1. The market leader (sets customer expectations)
2. The fastest-growing challenger
3. The cheapest / free option
4. The most-niche / specialized option

---

## Phase 2 — Feature Matrix

Create a structured comparison table:

| Feature | Your Product | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Core feature 1 | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | — |
| Core feature 2 | ✓ | — | ✓ | ✓ |
| Differentiator 1 | ✓ | — | — | — |
| Integration: Slack | ✓ | ✓ | — | ✓ |
| Mobile app | — | ✓ | ✓ | — |
| API access | ✓ | ✓ | — | — |
| SSO / Enterprise | — | ✓ | ✓ | — |

**Scoring rules:**
- ✓ = Full native feature
- ~ = Partial / workaround
- — = Not available
- ⧖ = On roadmap (verify with release notes)

### Where to Get Feature Data
- Public product pages and feature lists
- YouTube demo videos and walkthroughs
- G2/Capterra review categories
- Help documentation structure (reveals what they think is important)
- Free trial or freemium account

---

## Phase 3 — Pricing Analysis

Build a normalized pricing table:

| Plan | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Free/Trial | None | Freemium (limited) | 14-day trial |
| Entry | $29/mo (1 user) | $19/mo (3 users) | $49/mo (5 users) |
| Growth | $79/mo (5 users) | $49/mo (10 users) | $99/mo (15 users) |
| Enterprise | Custom | Custom | $299/mo |

**Normalize to per-seat cost** for apples-to-apples comparison.

**Pricing signals to capture:**
- What is gated at each tier? (Storage, users, features, API calls)
- Is there a freemium model? What's its conversion hook?
- How is enterprise handled? (Contact sales = they monetize at volume)
- Annual vs. monthly discount (reveals how they retain customers)
- What triggers upgrade? (Usage limits, team size, feature unlock)

---

## Phase 4 — UX and Positioning Audit

### Website Audit (10 minutes per competitor)

Capture answers to:

1. **Hero headline:** What outcome do they promise in 5 words or fewer?
2. **Primary CTA:** What action do they optimize for? (Trial, demo, pricing)
3. **Social proof:** How do they handle testimonials? (Logos, quotes, case studies, metrics?)
4. **Objection handling:** What fears do they address on the homepage?
5. **ICP signal:** Who is the homepage written for? (Job titles, company sizes, industries mentioned)

### Onboarding Audit (if free trial available)

Walk through first 15 minutes as a new user:
- Time to first value (how many steps before something useful happens?)
- What do they want you to do first?
- What emails do they send in the first 48 hours?
- Where does the flow break or create confusion?

### Review Mining (G2, Capterra, App Store, Reddit)

Sort by **most recent**, then **1-star reviews**, then **3-star reviews**.

Extract:
- Top 3 praised capabilities (what they do better than expected)
- Top 3 recurring complaints (what frustrates real users)
- Feature requests mentioned more than twice
- The exact language customers use to describe their problem

---

## Phase 5 — Positioning Map

Create a 2x2 matrix with the two dimensions that matter most to your ICP:

```
Example axes:
- Ease of use vs. Feature depth
- Price vs. Enterprise readiness
- Self-serve vs. High-touch support
- Speed of setup vs. Customization
```

Plot all competitors. Find the quadrant with high demand and low competition — that's your positioning opportunity.

---

## Phase 6 — SWOT Summary

For each key competitor, fill out:

| | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-|-----------|-----------|
| **Opportunities (for you)** | Where their strength creates customer dependency you can disrupt | Where their weakness is a gap you can fill |
| **Threats (to you)** | Where their strength directly counters your positioning | Where their weakness might push them to copy your approach |

---

## Actionable Insights Output

Every teardown must end with explicit decisions, not just observations:

```
Positioning Decision:
"We will position against [competitor] by emphasizing [differentiator]
because their reviews show customers are frustrated by [pain point]."

Feature Decision:
"We will prioritize [feature] because it appears in 3 competitor offerings
and is the top G2 request in our category."

Pricing Decision:
"We will price at [X] because it undercuts [competitor] at the entry tier
while matching their growth tier features."

Messaging Decision:
"We will not use [term/claim] because [competitor A and B] own it.
Instead, our headline will anchor on [unique angle]."
```

---

## Teardown Checklist

- [ ] 5–10 competitors identified across all three tiers
- [ ] Feature matrix completed (direct comparison, normalized)
- [ ] Pricing normalized to per-seat or per-unit equivalent
- [ ] Homepage audit completed for each (headline, CTA, ICP, proof)
- [ ] At least 20 reviews mined per key competitor (G2/Capterra/Reddit)
- [ ] UX walkthrough completed for top 2–3 competitors
- [ ] Positioning map drawn with two meaningful axes
- [ ] SWOT completed for top 2 direct competitors
- [ ] Output includes at least 4 explicit actionable decisions
- [ ] Teardown dated (market conditions change every 6–12 months — schedule a refresh)
