# Pitch Deck Visual Design Guide

## Core Purpose

A pitch deck has 10–15 seconds per slide to communicate before investor attention drifts. Visuals must carry meaning, not just decorate. Every slide should be understood before the presenter speaks — the words then deepen, not explain.

---

## The Standard Pitch Narrative

The deck tells a story. Slides follow a causal chain:

1. **Cover**: Company name, one-line description, presenter, date
2. **Problem**: The pain that exists in the world today
3. **Solution**: How you specifically solve that pain
4. **Product**: What the product is / how it works
5. **Market**: How big is the addressable opportunity
6. **Traction**: Evidence that people want this / it works
7. **Business Model**: How you make money
8. **Go-to-Market**: How you reach customers
9. **Competition**: Where you sit in the landscape
10. **Team**: Why you are the right people to execute
11. **Ask**: How much you're raising and how you'll use it
12. **Appendix**: Deep-dives available on request

### Slide Economy Rule
If you cannot state the core message of a slide in 10 words, the slide is not ready. Write that 10-word message as the slide title.

## Consistent Visual Language

### Why Consistency Matters
Inconsistent design signals inconsistent thinking. Investors pattern-match professionalism. A deck with 4 different font sizes, 7 shades of blue, and 3 different chart styles reads as disorganized — even if the business is sound.

### The Visual Language System (define before starting)
- **Primary color**: Brand color, used for headlines, accents, CTAs
- **Secondary color**: Supporting tone, used for charts, icons, backgrounds
- **Neutral dark**: For body text (#1a1a1a or very dark gray — never pure black)
- **Neutral light**: For backgrounds, dividers (#f5f5f5 or very light gray)

- **Heading font**: Single font for all H1 and slide titles
- **Body font**: Single font for all supporting text
- **Data font**: Monospace or tabular-figure font for numbers and data

- **Icon set**: One consistent icon library throughout (never mix styles)
- **Photo style**: Documentary, product shots, or lifestyle — never all three
- **Chart style**: One defined chart palette and style used on all data slides

## Slide Layout Principles

### Grid and Structure
- Use a consistent grid: 12-column or simple 3-column for most slides
- Left-aligned text reads faster than centered
- Breathing room (whitespace) signals confidence — crowded slides signal anxiety
- Content area: leave 60–80px clear zone from all slide edges

### Visual Hierarchy Per Slide
1. **Claim** (slide title): What you need the investor to believe after this slide
2. **Evidence**: The visual, data, or diagram that proves the claim
3. **Context** (optional): Small supporting text that anticipates a question

Nothing else. Three layers maximum.

### The "One Thought" Rule
Each slide communicates exactly one idea. If you need to say "and also...", that's two slides.

## Problem Slide

### Visual Approaches
- **Relatable scenario**: Photograph or illustration of the pain moment
- **Status quo flow**: Process diagram showing how painful the current path is
- **Cost quantification**: Bold number showing the scale of the problem
- **Quote from target customer**: Pulls investors into lived experience

### Design Rules
- Problem should feel urgent but not overwrought
- Avoid abstract stock photos of stressed people
- Specific data (pain in numbers) outperforms vague statements
- Color temperature: slightly cooler or more neutral (warmth is for solution)

---

## Solution Slide

### Visual Approaches
- **Before/After comparison**: Side-by-side of world with and without you
- **Product screenshot**: Actual product showing the solution in action
- **Simple flow diagram**: 3–5 step process showing how you fix the problem
- **The "aha" illustration**: Single diagram that makes it click

### Design Rules
- Solution slide should visually contrast with problem slide
- Use brighter, warmer palette than the problem slide
- Product screenshots should be clean, high-resolution, and real
- Never use a wireframe here — investors need to see the real thing

---

## Market Slide

### The TAM/SAM/SOM Framework (Visually)
- **TAM** (Total Addressable Market): Outer circle/largest
- **SAM** (Serviceable Addressable Market): Middle
- **SOM** (Serviceable Obtainable Market): Inner — what you can win

Visualize as concentric circles or nested rectangles. Label each with dollar value.

### Market Sizing Principles
- Bottom-up math beats top-down ("2% of a $50B market" is lazy)
- Bottom-up: (number of customers) × (ACV) = real market estimate
- Show your calculation, not just the conclusion
- Time-bound the numbers: "In 2025, the US market is..."

### Chart Types for Market
- Concentric circles (TAM/SAM/SOM)
- Bar chart with growth trajectory (CAGR)
- Geographic heat map for regional markets

## Traction Slide

### Metrics That Matter to Investors
- **Revenue**: MRR/ARR, month-over-month growth
- **Customers**: Total, paying, enterprise
- **Retention/Churn**: Net Revenue Retention, logo retention
- **Usage**: DAU/MAU, engagement metrics (only if high)
- **Pipeline**: For pre-revenue companies

### Chart Design for Traction
- Revenue chart: bar chart (monthly) or line chart — always start from $0
- Growth rate: call it out as a number in bold above the chart ("147% YoY")
- Use hockey-stick scale only if organic — label inflection points ("Series A", "Launched X")
- Time axis: always label (Jan 2023 → present), never "Month 1, Month 2"

### Callout Numbers
Big, bold, isolated key metrics work hard:

```
[Large number prominently displayed]
Brief context label below
```

Use for: ARR, customers, NPS score, key usage metric.

## Competition Slide

### The Positioning Matrix (2×2 Grid)
- Define two axes that your product wins on
- Place competitors in honest positions
- Your dot should be clearly differentiated (upper right is common)
- Axes must be dimensions investors care about

### Rules for Positioning Matrix
- Never place yourself so far from competitors it seems delusional
- Choose axes that are genuinely meaningful, not constructed to make you win
- Name the competitors clearly — investors know who they are
- More than 8 dots becomes unreadable

### Alternative: Feature Comparison Table
- Rows: features or capabilities
- Columns: You + 3–5 competitors
- Check marks vs. X marks (no partial credit — keep it binary or add nuance with color)
- Your column: highlighted in brand color

## Team Slide

### Standard Team Layout
- Photo (professional, consistent style across team)
- Name (bold)
- Title (smaller)
- 2–3 credential bullets (relevant prior experience, exit, degree)
- Optional: LinkedIn icon link

### Design Rules
- Photos at same crop, same size, same style
- Do not use LinkedIn thumbnail photos — shoot or source proper headshots
- 3–5 people maximum on the team slide; advisors in appendix
- Prior company logos as visual shorthand (Google, McKinsey, etc.)

---

## Ask Slide

### Structure
- **Raise amount**: Large, prominent
- **Use of funds**: Simple breakdown (not exhaustive line items)
- **Runway**: "18 months of runway" or "Reach [milestone] by [date]"
- **Key milestones**: What the money unlocks (Series A readiness)

### Visual Approaches
- Horizontal bar divided by use of funds category (% with labels)
- Simple 3-column: Amount | Purpose | Milestone
- Timeline showing: Seed → milestones → Series A trigger

### What Not to Include
- Financial projections with 5-year precision (no one believes them)
- Monthly burn breakdown (appendix, not main deck)
- Equity percentages offered (term sheet conversation)

---

## Data Presentation Rules

### Chart Styling
- Remove all chart borders and boxes
- Background: transparent or slide background color
- Grid lines: light gray, horizontal only
- Axis labels: small, neutral color, no units in every label (put units in title)
- Tick marks: remove
- Legend: replace with direct labels when possible

### Numbers in Slides
- Round aggressively: "$4.2M ARR" not "$4,187,432 ARR"
- Highlight key number with color, size, or bold — not all numbers equally
- Unit labels once (on axis), not repeated on every data point
- Growth rates in bold callouts above or beside the chart

---

## Typography in Pitch Decks

### Size Scale
- Slide title: 36–48px
- Body text / bullet points: 20–24px (never smaller — investors view at distance)
- Data labels: 16–20px
- Source / footnote: 12–14px

### Bullet Point Rules
- Maximum 5 bullets per slide
- Maximum 8 words per bullet
- Bullets support the spoken word; they do not replace it
- Fragments are fine — complete sentences waste space

---

## Final Quality Checklist

- [ ] Each slide has a single, clear message in the title
- [ ] Consistent font family and size scale throughout
- [ ] Consistent color palette — no rogue colors
- [ ] All charts start at zero (bar/column charts)
- [ ] Numbers rounded appropriately
- [ ] Team photos consistent style
- [ ] Logo visible on every slide (subtle, footer or corner)
- [ ] Slide numbers in footer for reference during Q&A
- [ ] Exported as PDF with embedded fonts
- [ ] Dark background version available (some investors view in dark mode)
- [ ] Tested at projector resolution (1920×1080) and screen (1440×900)
- [ ] Appendix prepared for deep-dive questions
